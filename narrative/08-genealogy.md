# Genealogy: how three traditions found the same lattice

The arithmetic story in this repository reads, by design, as if the Barnes–Wall lattice were the natural setting for Clifford+*T* quantum computation. The presentation theorem in [`02-presentation.md`](02-presentation.md) makes the prop of lattice automorphisms equal to the phased Clifford prop on the nose. The logical-lattice theorem in [`03-logical-lattice.md`](03-logical-lattice.md) makes stabilizer codes scaled copies of the same lattice. The grade in [`06-grade.md`](06-grade.md) prices magic by lattice depth.

It fits too well. A reader is entitled to ask whether the lattice has been chosen post hoc to look like the answer. The honest reply is no, and the reply has a structural form: every piece of this story — the lattice, the recursion, the Möbius fold, the cyclotomic tower, the identification with the Clifford group — was invented for an unrelated purpose decades before quantum information could even ask for it. The convergence is real, it is independent across communities, and there are five papers (one in 1937, one in 1959, one in 1961, one in 1964, and one in 2024) without which the arithmetic chapters would not be possible. This document tells that story.

Two things follow. First, the program's reliability is much higher than a one-paper synthesis would have: when three independent traditions all pick the same lattice as their natural object, without coordinating, across sixty-five years, that is strong extra-mathematical evidence that the lattice is a real natural kind rather than an artefact of how we chose to ask the question. Second, the lineage points sharply at the next move: every time in the program's history a community has stopped talking to its neighbours, the work has stalled; the next paper writes itself from the side that hasn't been read yet.

## Three independent traditions (1937–1990)

### Tradition I (1937, the algorithmic-combinatorics root)

F. Yates published in 1937, in the context of analysis of variance, an algorithm for evaluating a function on the subset lattice $2^{[n]}$ via a recursive single-coordinate sweep. The algorithm is layered Möbius inversion: at each step, peel one coordinate, store partial sums, recurse. The resulting transform sends $f : 2^{[n]} \to \mathbb{C}$ to its down-set Möbius coefficients
```math
m_U(f) = \sum_{V \subseteq U} (-1)^{|U|-|V|} f(V)
```
in time $O(n \cdot 2^n)$ instead of the naive $O(4^n)$. Yates was thinking about factorial experimental designs; the algorithm reads now as the canonical layered transform on the Boolean lattice. The down-set Möbius transform that powers the multi-monomial closed form in [`06-grade.md`](06-grade.md) is the same one.

### Tradition II (1959–1988, the geometry-of-numbers root)

E. S. Barnes and G. E. Wall published "Some extreme forms defined in terms of abelian groups" in the *Journal of the Australian Mathematical Society* 1 (1959). The paper sits in the Mordell–Davenport–Mahler school: it constructs positive-definite quadratic forms whose Hermite ratio achieves a local maximum, with the unusual feature that the forms are built as cosets indexed by elementary abelian 2-groups. The collaboration's distinctive feature was the pairing — Barnes the geometry-of-numbers half, Wall the finite-group-theory half. The forms they constructed had unusually large automorphism groups for the simple reason that Wall designed them to. Barnes and Wall did not coin the term "Barnes–Wall lattice"; the name was attached retroactively.

Three things should be noted about this paper. They are not doing coding theory. They are not doing quantum — the field will not exist for thirty years. And the headline construction, in dimension 16, gives a sphere packing of kissing number 4320 that is still the densest known.

Broué and Enguehard independently rediscovered the family in 1973, this time emphasising the automorphism group. Conway and Sloane's *Sphere Packings, Lattices and Groups* (Springer, 1988; 3rd ed. 1999) canonised the BW family in the sphere-packing record table. Forney's "Coset Codes II" (1988) articulated the Reed–Muller-to-Barnes–Wall dictionary via the Plotkin $(u, u+v)$ recursion of Plotkin (1960) — Morris Plotkin's 1960 binary-code construction, which combines two codes $C_1, C_2 \subseteq \mathbb{F}_2^n$ as $\{(u, u+v) : u \in C_1, v \in C_2\}$ to produce a length-$2n$ code of minimum distance $\min(2d_1, d_2)$; iterating gives Reed–Muller codes, and Forney's lattice lift gives the Barnes–Wall family. (Not to be confused with the algebraic-effect handlers of Gordon Plotkin.) By the early 1990s the lattice was a known object inside coding theory and sphere packing, but no one in either community thought it had anything to do with quantum mechanics, because no one in either community was thinking about quantum mechanics.

### Tradition III (1961, the finite-group-theory root)

Bolt, Room, and Wall published "On the Clifford collineation, transform and similarity groups" in three parts in the *Journal of the Australian Mathematical Society* in 1961. The same G. E. Wall who two years earlier had built the lattice now identified its automorphism group as a specific extraspecial-2 normaliser in $U(2^m)$, named after W. K. Clifford. This is the original "Cliff = Aut(BW)" theorem, decades before stabilizer codes existed; the Clifford group was a finite-group-theory object with no quantum-mechanical interpretation. The identification sat unused on the algebra side for forty years.

## The bridge years (1997–2010) and the long silence

The bridge from the three roots to quantum information assembled in the late 1990s at Bell Labs, AT&T, and Eindhoven — the same cluster of people, working in adjacent rooms, finally read the three traditions at once.

Calderbank, Cameron, Kantor, and Seidel published "$\mathbb{Z}_4$-Kerdock codes, orthogonal spreads, and extremal Euclidean line-sets" in *Proc. London Math. Soc.* 75 (1997). The paper restated the Bolt–Room–Wall Clifford-group identification in a coding-theoretic frame, threaded through Kerdock codes and orthogonal spreads, and made the Clifford collineation group visible to the coding theorist. Hammons, Kumar, Calderbank, Sloane, and Solé had laid the groundwork three years earlier with the $\mathbb{Z}_4$-linearity paper (1994), which exposed the Gray map and made the ring-theoretic structure of Kerdock-type codes available.

Then in 1998 the same authors (with Shor) published the canonical stabilizer-codes paper: Calderbank, Rains, Shor, and Sloane, "Quantum error correction via codes over GF(4)," *IEEE Trans. IT* 44. CRSS 1998 made the stabilizer/code dictionary that defines the field of quantum error correction. *It does not mention Barnes–Wall by name.* The CRSS framing was symplectic — additive self-orthogonal codes over GF(4) — and the lattice connection had not yet been drawn.

Nebe, Rains, and Sloane published "The invariants of the Clifford groups" in *Designs, Codes and Cryptography* 24 (2001). The abstract is unambiguous: *"The automorphism group of the Barnes–Wall lattice $L_m$ in dimension $2^m$ ($m \ne 3$) is a subgroup of index 2 in a certain Clifford group $C_m$,"* with quantum codes listed (alongside Siegel modular forms, Kerdock sets, and spherical designs) as one motivation. NRS 2001 is the first paper to state "Cliff = Aut(BW)" in the post-CRSS era, while citing the Bolt–Room–Wall original. The 2006 NRS book *Self-Dual Codes and Invariant Theory* (Springer) consolidated the result via the Clifford–Weil-group machinery.

The bridge was open in plain sight, and almost no one walked across it for two decades. The quantum-information literature stayed on the unitary side, with the smallest-denominator exponent of Ross–Selinger (2016) on $\mathbb{Z}[1/\sqrt{2}, i]$ as the natural complexity measure. Giles–Selinger 2013 characterised exact Clifford+*T* realisability as "entries in $\mathbb{Z}[1/\sqrt{2}, i]$." Amy, Glaudell, and Ross (2020) identified the Gaussian Clifford+*T* subgroup. None of these papers mention Barnes–Wall lattices.

Why the silence? Two plausible reasons. The lattice community published in *Designs, Codes and Cryptography* and *Journal of Number Theory*; the synthesis community in *Physical Review A* and the CS-theory conferences. They shared no journals and few referees. And the natural complexity measure on the synthesis side — the smallest-denominator exponent, an integer-valued valuation on $\mathbb{Z}[1/\sqrt{2}, i]$ — was already adequate for the questions people were asking. Until someone wanted a *graded* stratification of magic, and that question came from a different direction entirely.

## The convergence (2015–2024)

The convergence happened in part because of programming-language theory. The $\Pi$ / $\sqrt{\Pi}$ lineage (Sabry et al., 2012–) treats reversible classical computation as the symmetric monoidal closure of finite Boolean bijections, and quantum computation as the lift obtained by adding two square-root generators: $V$ (a square root of $X$) and $\omega = \zeta_8$ (the global eighth-root-of-unity phase). Carette, Heunen, Kaarsgaard, and Sabry's POPL paper (2024) proved that three equational axioms — $\omega^8 = \mathrm{id}$, $V^2 = \sigma_\oplus$, and $V \circ S \circ V = \omega^2 \cdot S \circ V \circ S$ — are complete for Clifford+*T* at one and two qubits.

The PL-side questions — what does it mean to add a square root, what is the cost of placing a phase under a sum type, why do we need exactly the cyclotomic integers — turn out to have *arithmetic* answers, and the arithmetic happens on the Barnes–Wall lattice. The grade in [`06-grade.md`](06-grade.md) is a $\lambda$-adic valuation on $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_8]$; magic is denominators; the *T*-count of a circuit is bounded below by its grade.

Kliuchnikov and Schönnenbeck published "Stabilizer operators and Barnes–Wall lattices" in *Quantum* (2024). The paper does three things at once. **(i)** Cyclotomic generalisation: BRW handled real, NRS extended to complex, K–S extended to every $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_{2^m})$ for $m \ge 2$. **(ii)** Stabilizer states are minimal lattice vectors: K–S Theorem 3.4 characterises the minimal vectors of the $n$-qubit BW lattice as the stabilizer states up to a fixed scaling. This is the missing direction NRS 2001 did not have; it is what turns the BW lattice from "the lattice with the right symmetry group" into a *semantic target* for circuit synthesis. **(iii)** Quantum-native proof: the proof uses Pauli expectations via trace pairings and the Choi correspondence, rather than modular forms or spherical designs. It is accessible to the quantum-information community.

By 2024 the bridge is no longer hidden. The four-citation attribution for "Cliff = Aut(BW)" is Bolt–Room–Wall 1961 (original group-theoretic identification, pre-quantum) → CCKS 1997 (coding-theoretic restatement, made the bridge quantum-relevant) → NRS 2001 (invariant-theoretic consolidation, first explicit statement in the quantum era) → Kliuchnikov–Schönnenbeck 2024 (cyclotomic generalisation, stabilizer-states-as-minimal-vectors, quantum-native proof). CRSS 1998 is sometimes cited reflexively for this identification but it should not be: CRSS made the stabilizer/code dictionary, not the lattice one.

## Where the repository's theorems sit in the lineage

Every result in this repository has a name in the genealogy: which tradition supplied the object, which paper made the object usable, and what the local work added beyond what was previously available.

- **Presentation theorem** ([`02-presentation.md`](02-presentation.md), $\mathfrak{BW}(n,n) = \langle\zeta\rangle \cdot \mathcal{C}_n$): assembled from K–S 2024 (lattice automorphism characterisation), Selinger 2015 (circuit-theoretic presentation), and Backens 2014 (ZX-calculus). The genealogy reads: this is the modern restatement of Bolt–Room–Wall 1961, with the cyclotomic generalisation of K–S 2024 and Selinger's circuit-theoretic presentation glued on top. What is added is the Lambek leg of the three-leg correspondence (programs = Clifford circuits; categories = lattice automorphism props; logic = Pauli logic of [`05-pauli-logic.md`](05-pauli-logic.md)).

- **Logical-lattice theorem** ([`03-logical-lattice.md`](03-logical-lattice.md), stabilizer codes are scaled Barnes–Wall lattices): kernel-checked. The genealogy supplied the lattice but not the code-lattice scaling. The contribution is to identify the precise scale: imposing one bit of stabilizer classicality costs exactly one factor of $(1+i)$ in the lattice.

- **$\lambda$-adic grade** ([`06-grade.md`](06-grade.md)): owes Ross–Selinger (2016) for the synthesis-side ancestor (smallest-denominator exponent on $\mathbb{Z}[1/\sqrt{2}, i]$); owes Amy–Glaudell–Ross (2020) for the Gaussian Clifford+*T* characterisation that pins the even-grade stratum to the Galois fixed field. What is added is the *grading* reading of these prior facts as a *filtration*, with grade-0 equal to Clifford by the presentation theorem, and the *T*-count bound as the operational price.

- **One ramified step per square root** ($(1 - \zeta_{16})^2 = (1 - \zeta_8) \cdot u'$): pure cyclotomic arithmetic; the kernel discharge is two-line. The lattice-side image of CHKRS catalytic embedding $\Phi_k : \Pi_k \to \Pi_{k-1}$, one rung at a time. The result was sitting in Forest 2015 (Clifford-cyclotomic gate rings) and earlier; the local reading is as the price for adding a square root in the calculus.

- **BW₂ diagonal grade table** ([`Arithmetic/Roots/BW2.lean`](../lean/Arithmetic/Roots/BW2.lean), kernel-checked): produced exact values $g(CZ) = 0$, $g(T \otimes T) = 2$, $g(\mathrm{c}T) = 3$ where the prior literature had only conjectured upper bounds. This is the first table where the lattice grade was *computed* rather than estimated.

- **Strict-subset linear closed form at every $n$** ([`StrictSubsetLowerBoundAllN.lean`](../lean/Arithmetic/Roots/StrictSubsetLowerBoundAllN.lean)): the sub-coordinate conductor generalises Kliuchnikov–Schönnenbeck's corner conductor from $d = n$ to every $d \le n$. New mathematics; the lattice tradition supplied the corner, the local work supplied the generalisation.

- **Multi-monomial Möbius closed form** ([`MultimonomialClosedForm.lean`](../lean/Arithmetic/Roots/MultimonomialClosedForm.lean), kernel-verified at $n \le 5$, all-$n$ routed): the keystone theorem of the grade story. The down-set Möbius transform that powers it has been in algorithmic combinatorics since Yates 1937, abstracted by Rota 1964, made a CS-theory workhorse by Björklund–Husfeldt–Kaski–Koivisto 2007. The recognition that the Plotkin $(u, u+v)$ recursion that defines the Barnes–Wall lattice *is* the Yates layered Möbius recursion in disguise — and therefore that the down-set Möbius transform is the unique linear-functional family whose restriction-and-extension commutation matches the lattice's pair-split — is the local structural contribution. See the Möbius angle below.

- **Cross-level grade table** ([`CrossLevelSelfSimilarity.lean`](../lean/Arithmetic/Roots/CrossLevelSelfSimilarity.lean)): grounds the level-3 / level-4 grade table in the separately-proved level-3 grade $g_3(T) = 1$, via the level-raising identity. Cross-level self-similarity rather than the false "grade halving" picture an earlier conjecture predicted.

- **Direct $n = 2$ and $n = 3$ Clifford-transport closure** ([`BW2Transport.lean`](../lean/LogicalLatticeTransport/BW2Transport.lean), [`BW3Transport.lean`](../lean/LogicalLatticeTransport/BW3Transport.lean)): cited for general $n$ in K–S 2024; closed here directly by kernel computation at the headline cases, with concrete generators rather than appeals to general lattice theory.

The kernel-checked content of the repository is genuinely *additive* to the lineage. The catalogue above identifies what was prior art, what was assembly, and what was new mathematics.

## The Möbius angle: an 89-year-old tool, found in 2026

The right tool was sitting on the algorithmic-combinatorics shelf for 89 years; the recognition that quantum needed it, not Walsh–Hadamard, was non-obvious because every prior quantum instinct points to Walsh.

Yates's 1937 algorithm for evaluating a function on the subset lattice is a single layered Möbius inversion. Rota's 1964 paper abstracted the classical divisor Möbius function and the Boolean inclusion-exclusion as two instances of a single poset construction, with Möbius inversion as the canonical change-of-basis. Björklund, Husfeldt, Kaski, and Koivisto (STOC 2007) made the down-set Möbius transform the standard tool for fast subset convolution in CS theory — a decisive theoretical-computer-science workhorse that is, despite this, structurally invisible in the mainstream stabilizer / magic / discrete-Wigner literature.

The Pauli decomposition of an operator is fundamentally the Walsh–Hadamard transform on the Pauli group (the symplectic Fourier on $(\mathbb{Z}/2)^{2n}$). The discrete Wigner function of Gross (2006) and the magic measures of Howard, Wallman, Veitch, and Emerson (2014) are character-theoretic, built on phase-point operators that are again Pauli-Fourier images. The stabilizer Rényi entropy sums against the same Walsh transform. In each of these, the down-set Möbius transform is structurally absent.

**Why the down-set version was the right object.** The Plotkin $(u, u+v)$ recursion that defines the Barnes–Wall lattice is, structurally, a down-set move: the $v$-component of a generator at coordinate set $U$ only sees Boolean points inside $U$, recursively. A linear-functional family on $f : 2^{[n]} \to \mathbb{C}$ that wants to *commute* with this recursion must be a sum over the order ideal of $U$, never a sum over the full cube. The down-set Möbius transform is the unique such family: at each Plotkin step, the Möbius coefficient at $U$ in the depth-$(n+1)$ vector equals the Möbius coefficient at $U$ on the low slice (if $U$ avoids the last coordinate) or on the slice difference (if $U$ contains it). These are the naturality squares of the Möbius functor with respect to the lattice's pair-split. The Walsh–Hadamard transform, which sums over the full cube with signs from a symplectic form, does not have this property; the catalogue of refuted closed-form candidates from earlier rounds is the explicit witness.

The down-set Möbius was the right object waiting to be found. It had been sitting in algorithmic combinatorics for nearly a century, and in belief / Dempster–Shafer theory, multivariate mutual information, and Stanley–Reisner theory for decades. The recognition that the Barnes–Wall lattice asks for it is what the multi-monomial closed form contributes. The price of recognition was a multi-tradition convergence; the price of *not* recognising for ninety years was that quantum information re-invented the Walsh side three times (Pauli decomposition, discrete Wigner, stabilizer Rényi entropy) without noticing it had a sibling.

## Future scope: where the lineage points next

Every time in the program's history a community has stopped talking to its neighbours, the work has stalled; the next paper writes itself from the side that hasn't been read yet. Three directions follow from the genealogy.

**Positive-grade Clifford+*T* completeness at $n \ge 3$.** The open equational-completeness problem of Heunen–Kaarsgaard for higher-qubit Clifford+*T* is structurally the missing piece of the arithmetic story here. The lineage says the answer will come from someone who reads Bolt–Room–Wall 1961 and the current rig-groupoid work in the same week. The grade is the invariant that stratifies the problem (grade 0 = stabilizer, even grade = Gaussian Clifford+*T*, odd positive grade = the genuinely-quantum residue); the catalytic embedding supplies the per-level inductive lift; the multi-monomial closed form supplies the per-grade enumeration of diagonal phases. The next direction is a grade-stratified inductive completeness with the closed form in hand.

**The Pauli-path / noisy-simulation bridge.** The classical-simulation-of-noisy-circuits programme of Aharonov–Gao–Landau–Liu–Vazirani (2023), Schuster–Yin–Gao–Yao (2024), and Angrisani–Mele–Rudolph–Cerezo–Holmes (2024) uses Pauli-weight contraction inequalities to truncate Heisenberg-picture sums. The BW grade is a parallel arithmetic invariant of the same operator, controlled by the down-set Möbius transform of the diagonal-phase fragment. Two communities, two transforms, one Boolean cube — exactly the pattern that produces the third paper.

**The categorical / $\Pi$-side completion.** The CHKRS catalytic-embedding induction closes the Gaussian Clifford+*T* stratum at every cyclotomic level $k$. The lineage suggests that the positive-grade extension will use a similar Gray-induction machinery, but stratified by grade rather than level. The cross-level table is grounded in $g(T) = 1$; the next direction is the cross-grade analogue, with the multi-monomial closed form supplying the per-grade equation set.

The arithmetic story in this repository is the discovery that several lineages converge on one lattice. The pattern of the convergence is informative: every time a community has missed the bridge, the missed bridge has been the next research project; every time a community has crossed, the result has been a kernel-checkable theorem. The genealogy is not just historical record but *predictive evidence*: the bridges that remain uncrossed — between the lattice side and the noisy-simulation side, between the catalytic embedding and the grade-stratified completeness, between the down-set Möbius and the Walsh–Hadamard — are exactly where the next results will live. The lineage continues because the convergences keep happening.
