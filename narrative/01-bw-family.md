# The Barnes–Wall family

## Setup

Work over the Gaussian integers $R = \mathbb{Z}[i]$, with the prime $\varphi = 1 + i$ — the unique prime above two, of field norm $|\varphi|^2 = 2$. The *n*-qubit Barnes–Wall lattice $\mathrm{BW}\_{n}$ is the rank-$2^n$ free *R*-module on $(\mathbb{C}^2)^{\otimes n}$ generated by the columns of $B^{\otimes n}$, where
```math
B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + i & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \varphi & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.
```
Equivalently $\mathrm{BW}\_{n} = B^{\otimes n}\,R^{2^n} \subset \mathbb{C}^{2^n}$.

That definition gives three things for free, and one thing not for free.

## Three immediate properties

The basis matrices tensor on the nose, so $\mathrm{BW}_{m+n} = \mathrm{BW}\_{m} \otimes_R \mathrm{BW}\_{n}$. Tensor products of lattice vectors are lattice vectors of the bigger lattice, no associator bookkeeping required. Every prop-flavored statement downstream consumes this.

The columns of *B* span $R^2$ freely. The first column is $\varphi |0\rangle$, the Z-eigenvector $|0\rangle$ scaled by the prime; the second is $|0\rangle + |1\rangle$, the unnormalized X-eigenvector $|+\rangle$. Tensor this with itself *n* times and every vector $w \in \mathrm{BW}\_{n}$ has a unique decomposition
```math
w = \varphi|0\rangle \otimes a \;+\; \bigl(|0\rangle + |1\rangle\bigr) \otimes b, \qquad a, b \in \mathrm{BW}_{n - 1}.
```
This *free-module decomposition* is what makes lattice membership a recursion: if we write $w = [w_0, w_1]$ for the two halves in $\mathbb{C}^{2^{n-1}}$, then $w \in \mathrm{BW}\_{n}$ if and only if $w_0 \in \mathrm{BW}_{n-1}$ and $w_1 - w_0$ is $\varphi$ times something also in $\mathrm{BW}_{n-1}$. We use this everywhere — in the membership test, in the decoder, in the logical-lattice theorem.

The two columns of *B* are not arbitrary, and a reader from the categorical-quantum-mechanics tradition will recognise them at sight. Each column is a representative of one of the qubit's two complementary classical structures: the Z-basis and the X-basis, viewed as commutative special $\dagger$-Frobenius monoids in the sense of Coecke–Pavlović–Vicary. The asymmetry — only the Z-column carries a factor of $\varphi$ — is exactly what makes Z-axis classicality cost something in the lattice, and X-axis classicality cost nothing. We will see in [`03-logical-lattice.md`](03-logical-lattice.md) that one bit of Z-stabilizer classicality costs exactly $\varphi$, no more, no less.

## The deep input, used freely

What does not follow from the definition is the third structural property: the minimal vectors of $\mathrm{BW}\_{n}$ are exactly the scaled stabilizer states. Concretely, every minimal vector has squared norm $2^n$, and is of the form $\zeta^k \cdot \varphi^n \cdot C|0\rangle^{\otimes n}$ for some Clifford $C$ and some eighth root $\zeta^k$. There are exactly $|\mathrm{Stab}\_{n}| \cdot 4$ of them. This is the content of Kliuchnikov–Schönnenbeck 2024, Theorem 3.4, with Calderbank as the historical antecedent. At *n* = 1, 2, 3 the counts come out as $24, 240, 4320$ — the kissing numbers of $D_4$, $E_8$, and the classical Barnes–Wall lattice in sixteen real dimensions. Pleasingly, those are the kissing numbers a lattice theorist would write down without ever mentioning a qubit; the same numbers come out either way.

## The Hadamard convention

The textbook Hadamard
```math
H = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt 2}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 1 & -1\end{pmatrix}
```
has irrational entries, which means it is not in our ring. The lattice-preserving normalization is the rephased
```math
\tilde H = \zeta_8^{-1}\,H = \tfrac{1 - i}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 1 & -1\end{pmatrix},
```
with entries in $\mathbb{Q}(i)$. Paired with $S = \mathrm{diag}(1, i)$ and $\mathrm{CNOT}$, this $\tilde H$ generates the Clifford group as a group of lattice automorphisms. The leftover phase $\zeta^k$ in the minimal-vector formula above is where the missing factor goes. We make this convention once and use it everywhere.

## What's new here, what's in the literature

The construction of the lattice tower is Barnes and Wall's, going back to 1959; the modern read over the cyclotomic rings is Forest–Gosset–Kliuchnikov–McKinnon (2015). The minimal-vector identification with stabilizer states is Kliuchnikov–Schönnenbeck (2024), drawing on Calderbank (1997). The free-module decomposition itself is a one-line consequence of how we have written the basis; what we do with it — the logical-lattice theorem, the decoder reading, the Pauli-logic semantics — is the contribution of this work.
